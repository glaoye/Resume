Investigating Methods for Cooling and Trapping Atoms with Hyperfine Structure

Show More Show Less

Summary: The focus of this report was to discuss how, and under what conditions, atoms with hyperfine structure are cooled and trapped. With Caesium used as the main example, I deduced the optimal wavelengths at which Caesium can be trapped without producing inconveniences in later uses, such as quantum computing and a variety of experiments. I also deduced the lowest temperature that Caesium can reach when subjected to an optical lattice, by producing a computer simulation to mimic laboratory conditions for an optical lattice.

Measuring the Specific Rotation of a Sucrose Solution and Verdet Constant of a Salt Solution

Show More Show Less

Summary: This investigation involved finding the specific rotation (the ability of a material to rotate polarised light) of sucrose solution, by measuring the phase shift of a rotating plane polarised laser beam with various concentrations of sucrose. There was also an attempt to measure the Verdet constant (the ability of a magnetic field to rotate polarised light propagating through a substance) of a salt solution - however the results were inconclusive due to not having access to a large enough magnetic field. Despite encountering many challenges along the way, the experiment was an enjoyable and thought-provoking one.

Programming

Here are some of my favourite programming tasks I have completed, either as part of my degree or just for fun

Optimising Light Paths

Show More Show Less

This was my first programming project, assigned to me in Year 1, and what ultimately sparked my initial passion for software development. The requirements were to produce a programme that would track the paths of multiple light rays as they traversed through linear and spherical lenses of different shapes and orientations, before landing on a detector. The extension task was to produce a function that would optimise the number of rays falling within a specified radius on the detector by changing only the radii of curvature of the spherical lenses. This project was arguably the largest academic challenge I had to overcome in my degree. Training in programming was very limitted and entire concepts such as functions, loops, modules and objects were either unknown or a complete blur at first. However, I rose to the challenge and revelled in the opportunity to learn new things. I am very proud of the result.

Random Walks

Show More Show Less

Along with my main programming projects, I also completed multiple smaller programming tasks in my spare time over the years. One of my favourites to research and implement was Random Walks - a task given to me by my level 2 Computing supevisor, after my request for more programming challenges. This required me to produce a programme that would track and map the path of bacterium as they converge towards areas with the highest food density. The Chaotic Nature of the Newton Raphson Method

Show More Show Less

This was a research challenge I requested from my computing supervisor. It involved using the Newton Raphson method over 1000 iterations to find the roots in the complex plane of the 4th order. The programme demonstrated that plotting the outcome of each iteration produces fractals - a never ending, infinetly complex pattern, that are self-similar accross all scales. Such patterns are a demonstration of natural chaos, occuring in scenes such as trees, mountains and clouds.

Music Applications

Show More Show Less

As I am ever looking for ways to unite my creative passions with my technology ones, I am planning and building an application that will write sheet music based on the notes that are played/sang into it. The idea came into fruition as I am constantly trying to write sheet music for songs that I write, which is more exhausting than one may think. The Eureka moment - why not create an app that can do it for me? I am not only producing this app out of requirement, or even for love of music, but also to challenge myself.
The next step after this would be to produce a web platform where musicians (professional and aspiring) can upload tracks and sheet music and have other musicians make contributions, much like a musical GitHub. Suggesions from contributors would mostly be in the form of a track, which could overlay or append the original song. The original composer will have full control on which suggestions to add to their song. There will also be a voting system where tracks can be promoted and made more visible to the composer. Composers can send out requests if they are looking for a specific instrument to join. I would also like their to be the added feature of 'live jam sessions'.
Obviously this plan is ambitious, but that makes me ever more motivated to fufill it.
